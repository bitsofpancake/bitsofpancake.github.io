  • Perspectives on the variational autoencoder
  • There are many ways of looking at the variational autoencoder, or VAE, of Kingma et al. (2014), and the evidence lower bound, or ELBO, used to train it. The goal of this post is to concisely catalog these perspectives for quick reference.
  • In the VAE, there are two probability distributions:
  • q(x,z)=q(z∣x)q(x),p(x,z)=p(x∣z)p(z). q(x, z) = q(z|x) q(x), \qquad p(x, z) = p(x |z) p(z). q(x,z)=q(z∣x)q(x),p(x,z)=p(x∣z)p(z).
  • Conceptually, q(x)q(x)q(x) is the data distribution, making q(z∣x)q(z|x)q(z∣x) an encoder, and p(z)p(z)p(z) is a latent prior, making p(x∣z)p(x|z)p(x∣z) a decoder. These terms are assumed to be tractable, whereas reversed terms like q(x∣z)q(x|z)q(x∣z) and p(z∣x)p(z|x)p(z∣x) and marginalized terms like q(z)q(z)q(z) and p(x)p(x)p(x) are intractable.
  • The variational autoencoder is trained by maximizing the ELBO:
  • ELBO=Ex∼q(x)Ez∼q(z∣x)log⁡p(z)p(x∣z)q(z∣x)=Ex,z∼q(x,z)log⁡p(x,z)q(z∣x). \text{ELBO} = \E_{x \sim q(x)} \E_{z \sim q(z|x)} \log \frac{p(z) p(x | z)}{q(z|x)} = \E_{x,z \sim q(x,z)} \log \frac{p(x,z)}{q(z|x)}. ELBO=Ex∼q(x)​Ez∼q(z∣x)​logq(z∣x)p(z)p(x∣z)​=Ex,z∼q(x,z)​logq(z∣x)p(x,z)​.
  • Maximum likelihood. We can think of the VAE as training a generative model p(x)p(x)p(x) using maximum likelihood, by attempting to maximize Ex∼q(x)log⁡p(x)\E_{x \sim q(x)} \log p(x)Ex∼q(x)​logp(x). Since p(x)p(x)p(x) is intractable, we instead maximize a lower bound Ex∼q(x)log⁡p(x)≥ELBO=Ex∼q(x)log⁡p(x)−Ex∼q(x)D(q(z∣x)∣∣p(z∣x)), \E_{x \sim q(x)} \log p(x) \ge \text{ELBO} = \E_{x \sim q(x)} \log p(x) - \E_{x \sim q(x)} D(q(z|x) || p(z|x)), Ex∼q(x)​logp(x)≥ELBO=Ex∼q(x)​logp(x)−Ex∼q(x)​D(q(z∣x)∣∣p(z∣x)), a lower bound that is tight when q(z∣x)=p(z∣x)q(z|x) = p(z|x)q(z∣x)=p(z∣x).
  • Variational Bayes. From the perspective of Bayesian inference, p(z)p(z)p(z) is a prior, and p(x∣z)p(x|z)p(x∣z) is a likelihood. This makes p(z∣x)p(z|x)p(z∣x) the posterior; unfortunately, this is intractable, so we approximate it with a variational posterior q(z∣x)q(z|x)q(z∣x). Ideally, we would minimize Ex∼q(x)D(q(z∣x)∣∣p(z∣x))\E_{x \sim q(x)} D(q(z|x) || p(z|x))Ex∼q(x)​D(q(z∣x)∣∣p(z∣x)), but this is intractable, so we instead maximize ELBO=Ex∼q(x)[log⁡p(x)−D(q(z∣x)∣∣p(z∣x))]. \text{ELBO} = \E_{x \sim q(x)} [\log p(x) - D(q(z|x) || p(z|x))].   • Autoencoder. We can view the VAE as an autoencoder by writing the ELBO as ELBO=Ex∼q(x)Ez∼q(z∣x)log⁡p(x∣z)−Ex∼q(x)D(q(z∣x)∣∣p(z)). \text{ELBO} = \E_{x \sim q(x)} \E_{z \sim q(z|x)}\log p(x|z) - \E_{x \sim q(x)} D(q(z|x) || p(z)).   • Expectation-maximization. Expectation-maximization is an iterative algorithm for computing the maximum likelihood estimator. In the language of VAEs, it maximizes the ELBO by coordinate ascent, alternately on q(z∣x)q(z|x)q(z∣x) (the “E-step”) and on p(z)p(z)p(z) (the “M-step”). In the E-step, the ELBO is maximized when q(z∣x)q(z|x)q(z∣x) is set to p(z∣x)p(z|x)p(z∣x). (This is called the E-step because p(z∣x)=Eh∼p(h∣x)p(z∣x,h)p(z|x) = \E_{h\sim p(h|x)} p(z|x,h)p(z∣x)=Eh∼p(h∣x)​p(z∣x,h), where hhh represents unobserved data.) In the M-step, the ELBO is maximized when p(z)p(z)p(z) is set to δ(z−z∗)\delta(z - z^*)δ(z−z∗), where z∗z^*z∗ maximizes the likelihood p(x∣z)p(x|z)p(x∣z).
  • Representation learning. The encoder q(z∣x)q(z|x)q(z∣x) in a VAE can be seen as a way to learn a compact representation of the data. However, the ELBO on its own does not seem to promote this objective particularly well. See, for example, Higgins et al. (2017) and Alemi et al. (2018).
