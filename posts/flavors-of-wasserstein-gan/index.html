  • Flavors of Wasserstein GAN
  • Arkjovsky et al. (2017) introduced the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN), a formulation of GANs (generative adversarial networks) that attempts to minimize the Wasserstein distance WWW between the generator’s distribution and the data distribution. It relies on one particularly useful expression for this distance, the Kantorovich-Rubinstein formula, which expresses the Wasserstein distance between two distributions P\mathbb{P}P and Q\mathbb{Q}Q as
  • W(P,Q)=sup⁡f∈Lip1Ex∼P[f(x)]−Ey∼Q[f(y)], W(\mathbb{P}, \mathbb{Q}) = \sup_{f \in \mathrm{Lip}_1} \E_{x \sim \mathbb{P}}[f(x)] - \E_{y \sim \mathbb{Q}}[f(y)], W(P,Q)=f∈Lip1​sup​Ex∼P​[f(x)]−Ey∼Q​[f(y)], where Lip1\mathrm{Lip}_1Lip1​ is the set of all 111-Lipschitz functions. We’ll talk about what it means for fff to be 111-Lipschitz later. Intuitively, all it means is that f(x)f(x)f(x) doesn’t change too quickly as xxx changes.
  • The WGAN algorithm
  • The WGAN algorithm works as follows. Let P\mathbb{P}P be the data distribution that we’re trying to mimic, and let Qθ\mathbb{Q}_\thetaQθ​ be the generated distribution, parameterized by θ\thetaθ. Usually, Qθ\mathbb{Q}_\thetaQθ​ is the distribution of Gθ(z)G_\theta(z)Gθ​(z), where GθG_\thetaGθ​ is some neural network that transforms a noise sample z∼N(0,I)z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)z∼N(0,I) into a sample. To train Qθ\mathbb{Q}_\thetaQθ​ to approximate P\mathbb{P}P, we approximately minimize the distance W(P,Qθ)W(\mathbb{P}, \mathbb{Q}_\theta)W(P,Qθ​). In particular, we use the approximation W^(P,Qθ)=E^x∼P[fϕ(x)]−E^y∼Qθ[fϕ(y)], \hat{W}(\mathbb{P}, \mathbb{Q}_\theta) = \hat\E_{x \sim \mathbb{P}}[f_\phi(x)] - \hat\E_{y \sim \mathbb{Q}_\theta}[f_\phi(y)], W^(P,Qθ​)=E^x∼P​[fϕ​(x)]−E^y∼Qθ​​[fϕ​(y)], where fϕf_\phifϕ​ is a critic, and E^\hat\EE^ denotes an approximation to the expectation by a finite sample. Notice that if fϕf_\phifϕ​ is indeed 111-Lipschitz, then by the Kantorovich-Rubinstein formula above, W(P,Qθ)≥E[W^(P,Qθ)]. W(\mathbb{P}, \mathbb{Q}_\theta) \ge \E[\hat{W}(\mathbb{P}, \mathbb{Q}_\theta)]. W(P,Qθ​)≥E[W^(P,Qθ​)]. In other words, W^(P,Qθ)\hat{W}(\mathbb{P}, \mathbb{Q}_\theta)W^(P,Qθ​) is a lower bound to the true Wasserstein distance in expectation.
  • The idea, then, is to maximize W^(P,Qθ)\hat{W}(\mathbb{P}, \mathbb{Q}_\theta)W^(P,Qθ​) with respect to fϕf_\phifϕ​ to get the best lower bound for the true Wasserstein distance, and then perform a gradient step on Qθ\mathbb{Q}_\thetaQθ​ to decrease our approximation W^(P,Qθ)\hat{W}(\mathbb{P}, \mathbb{Q}_\theta)W^(P,Qθ​), hopefully decreasing the true distance as a result.
  • Concretely, we perform the following steps:
    • Optimize critic. Perform gradient steps on ϕ\phiϕ to maximize E^x∼P[fϕ(x)]−E^y∼Qθ[fϕ(y)], \hat\E_{x \sim \mathbb{P}}[f_\phi(x)] - \hat\E_{y \sim \mathbb{Q}_\theta}[f_\phi(y)], E^x∼P​[fϕ​(x)]−E^y∼Qθ​​[fϕ​(y)], somehow constraining fϕf_\phifϕ​ to 111-Lipschitz functions, thus improving our approximation to the Wasserstein distance.
    • Optimize generator. Perform gradient steps on θ\thetaθ to minimize the approximate distance W^(P,Qθ)\hat{W}(\mathbb{P}, \mathbb{Q}_\theta)W^(P,Qθ​).
    • Repeat. Go to step 1.
  • Once θ\thetaθ is trained, the hope is that Qθ\mathbb{Q}_\thetaQθ​ will accurately model the data distribution P\mathbb{P}P.
  • The Lipschitz condition
  • One interesting degree of freedom in this algorithm is how to constrain fϕf_\phifϕ​ to a family of 111-Lipschitz functions. Recall that fff is called kkk-Lipschitz if ∣f(x)−f(y)∣≤k∣∣x−y∣∣|f(x) - f(y)| \le k ||x - y||∣f(x)−f(y)∣≤k∣∣x−y∣∣ for all x,yx, yx,y.
  • Notice that we can actually relax the Lipschitz condition a little: fff is 111-Lipschitz if and only if kfkfkf is kkk-Lipschitz, so optimizing fϕf_\phifϕ​ over kkk-Lipschitz functions instead of 111-Lipschitz functions simply scales the optimal fϕf_\phifϕ​ by kkk. Thus so long as kkk is fixed, nothing changes, except for the scale of the optimal fϕf_\phifϕ​. (This does affect the effective learning rate of the generator step.)
  • Let’s denote the Lipschitz constant of a function fff as ∣∣f∣∣Lip||f||_{\mathrm{Lip}}∣∣f∣∣Lip​, the smallest constant kkk for which fff is kkk-Lipschitz. (If we define ∣∣f∣∣Lip=k+∣f(0)∣||f||_{\mathrm{Lip}} = k + |f(0)|∣∣f∣∣Lip​=k+∣f(0)∣, then ∣∣f∣∣Lip||f||_{\mathrm{Lip}}∣∣f∣∣Lip​ actually becomes a true norm. In our application, we can safely assume fϕ(0)=0f_\phi(0) = 0fϕ​(0)=0, since we can always subtract an arbitrary constant; nothing changes because we only access fϕf_\phifϕ​ through differences or derivatives.)
  • The derivative
  • One way to control the Lipschitz constant of fϕf_\phifϕ​ is through its derivative. Specifically, if fff is continuous and differentiable, then fff is 111-Lipschitz if and only if ∣∣∇f∣∣≤1||\nabla f|| \le 1∣∣∇f∣∣≤1 everywhere.

If we define g(t)=f(tx+(1−t)y)g(t) = f(tx + (1-t)y)g(t)=f(tx+(1−t)y), then the mean value theorem says that there exists a ccc such that g′(c)=f(x)−f(y)g'(c) = f(x) - f(y)g′(c)=f(x)−f(y). But g′(c)=(x−y)⋅∇f(z)g'(c) = (x - y) \cdot \nabla f(z)g′(c)=(x−y)⋅∇f(z), where z=cx+(1−c)yz = cx + (1-c)yz=cx+(1−c)y.   • Conversely, suppose ∣∣∇f(z)∣∣>1||\nabla f(z)|| > 1∣∣∇f(z)∣∣>1 for some zzz.   • The spectral norm
  • Another way to control the Lipschitz constant of fϕf_\phifϕ​ is to take advantage of the particular structure of neural networks. If we assume that fϕf_\phifϕ​ is a neural network, so that fϕf_\phifϕ​ alternates affine transformations and nonlinearities fϕ(x)=gnWn⋯g2W2g1W1x, f_\phi(x) = g_n W_n \cdots g_2 W_2 g_1 W_1 x, fϕ​(x)=gn​Wn​⋯g2​W2​g1​W1​x, then since compositions of functions behave nicely with the Lipschitz norm, we may conclude that ∣∣fϕ∣∣Lip≤∣∣gn∣∣Lip∣∣Wn∣∣Lip⋯∣∣g2∣∣Lip∣∣W2∣∣Lip∣∣g1∣∣Lip∣∣W1∣∣Lip. ||f_\phi||_{\mathrm{Lip}} \le ||g_n||_{\mathrm{Lip}} ||W_n||_{\mathrm{Lip}} \cdots ||g_2||_{\mathrm{Lip}} ||W_2||_{\mathrm{Lip}} ||g_1||_{\mathrm{Lip}} ||W_1||_{\mathrm{Lip}}. ∣∣fϕ​∣∣Lip​≤∣∣gn​∣∣Lip​∣∣Wn​∣∣Lip​⋯∣∣g2​∣∣Lip​∣∣W2​∣∣Lip​∣∣g1​∣∣Lip​∣∣W1​∣∣Lip​.   • For the linear layers, remarkably we have that ∣∣W∣∣Lip=sup⁡x≠y∣∣Wx−Wy∣∣∣∣x−y∣∣=sup⁡z≠0∣∣Wz∣∣∣∣z∣∣=∣∣W∣∣op, ||W||_{\mathrm{Lip}} = \sup_{x \ne y} \frac{||Wx - Wy||}{||x- y||} = \sup_{z \ne 0} \frac{||Wz||}{||z||} = ||W||_{\text{op}}, ∣∣W∣∣Lip​=x≠ysup​∣∣x−y∣∣∣∣Wx−Wy∣∣​=z≠0sup​∣∣z∣∣∣∣Wz∣∣​=∣∣W∣∣op​, where ∣∣W∣∣op||W||_{\text{op}}∣∣W∣∣op​ is the operator norm. Furthermore, when ∣∣⋅∣∣||\cdot||∣∣⋅∣∣ is the Euclidean norm, the operator norm is called the spectral norm. The spectral norm of ∣∣W∣∣||W||∣∣W∣∣ is the largest singular value of the matrix WWW, usually denoted σ1(W)\sigma_1(W)σ1​(W). Therefore, to control the Lipschitz constant of a layer WWW, all we need to do is bound the largest singular value of WWW.
  • As for the nonlinearities, most in use are Lipschitz. The only ones I can think of that we need to be careful of is parametric ReLU; since the slope is trainable, it can have unbounded Lipschitz constant.
  • Algorithmic approachs
  • So far, there have been three main ways that have been proposed in the literature to enforce the Lipschitz constraint: weight clipping, gradient penalty, and spectral normalization.
  • Weight clipping
  • Arkjovsky et al. (2017) originally proposed weight clipping as a crude way to enforce the Lipschitz condition (a “clearly terrible way,” in their own words). After each gradient step on ϕ\phiϕ, they clip the weights ϕ\phiϕ to only take on values in [−c,c][-c, c][−c,c] for some constant ccc.
  • This is an example of the second approach, control with the spectral norm of each layer. The claim is that the family of neural networks with weights between [−c,c][-c, c][−c,c] is kkk-Lipschitz for some kkk. One neat way to see this is to notice that σ1(W)\sigma_1(W)σ1​(W) is the square root of the largest eigenvalue of WTWW^T WWTW, and the eigenvalues are bounded by m2c2m^2c^2m2c2 by the Gershgorin circle theorem, where WWW has mmm rows. Hence σ1(W)\sigma_1(W)σ1​(W) is bounded by mcmcmc, so the family of networks we’re optimizing over is kkk-Lipschitz for some kkk.
  • So why is it a clearly terrible way? The key is capacity underuse. Notice that the optimal function fϕf_\phifϕ​ will have a Lipschitz constant of exactly kkk, since otherwise we can scale our function to have Lipschitz constant kkk and achieve a larger value for the objective E^x∼P[fϕ(x)]−E^y∼Qθ[fϕ(y)]. \hat\E_{x \sim \mathbb{P}}[f_\phi(x)] - \hat\E_{y \sim \mathbb{Q}_\theta}[f_\phi(y)]. E^x∼P​[fϕ​(x)]−E^y∼Qθ​​[fϕ​(y)]. This means that effectively, the space of functions that we are optimizing over is limited to the space of functions with Lipschitz constant exactly kkk.
  • If we perform weight clipping, the space of functions with maximum Lipschitz constant is where each weight is ±c\pm c±c, achieving the bound mentioned above of mcmcmc. Gulrajani et al. (2017) found that training WGANs with weight clipping indeed leads to weight matrices populated by ±c\pm c±c. Such matrices are not very expressive and cannot discriminate well between real and fake samples.
  • Gradient penalty
  • As a gentler alternative, Gulrajani et al. (2017) proposed directly penalizing the gradient to have norm 111 by adding a regularization term λE^x^∼P^[(∣∣∇fϕ(x^)∣∣2−1)2] \lambda \hat\E_{\hat{x} \sim \hat{\mathbb{P}}}[ (|| \nabla f_\phi(\hat{x}) ||_2 - 1)^2] λE^x^∼P^​[(∣∣∇fϕ​(x^)∣∣2​−1)2] to the loss, where P^\hat\mathbb{P}P^ is the following distribution: let x^\hat{x}x^ is be a random point between x∼Px \sim \mathbb{P}x∼P and y∼Qθy \sim \mathbb{Q}_\thetay∼Qθ​, two random points from the data distribution and the generator distribution respectively.   • As mentioned above, constraining ∣∣∇fϕ∣∣||\nabla f_\phi ||∣∣∇fϕ​∣∣ to be at most 111 everywhere would constrain fϕf_\phifϕ​ to be Lipschitz. With this approach, however, we penalize the function to satisfy the Lipschitz condition only on straight lines connecting the support of P\mathbb{P}P with the support of Qθ\mathbb{Q}_\thetaQθ​. I believe that enforcing this weaker constraint is sufficient from the perspective of the Kantorovich-Rubinstein formula and optimal transport theory — if you have a proof, please let me know! A good reference is Villani’s Optimal Transport, Old and New.
  • I find Gulrajani et al.’s justification for why they penalize the gradient norm to be exactly 111 (rather than merely at most 111) to be less convincing, though. They cite a theorem that says that for the optimal f∗f^*f∗, ∣∣∇f∗(x^)∣∣=1|| \nabla f^*(\hat{x}) || = 1∣∣∇f∗(x^)∣∣=1 almost surely if (x,y)∼π(x, y) \sim \pi(x,y)∼π, where π\piπ is the optimal coupling between P\mathbb{P}P and Qθ\mathbb{Q}_\thetaQθ​. However, in their penalty, they sample from (x,y)∼P×Qθ(x,y) \sim \mathbb{P} \times \mathbb{Q}_\theta(x,y)∼P×Qθ​, whose support is much less sparse than π\piπ; it is not true generally that ∣∣∇f∗(x^)∣∣=1|| \nabla f^*(\hat{x}) || = 1∣∣∇f∗(x^)∣∣=1 P^\hat\mathbb{P}P^-almost everywhere. Thus the two-sided penalty seems a bit too heavy-handed.
  • Nonetheless, the gradient penalty has proven empirically successful at training GANs.
  • Spectral normalization
  • Miyato et al. (2018) propose a third alternative that takes advantage of the special structure of neural networks by bounding the spectral norm of each layer, as mentioned above.
  • Concretely, they take a normal network, but replace each layer WWW with W∣∣W∣∣2\frac{W}{||W||_2}∣∣W∣∣2​W​, normalizing each layer to automatically have spectral norm 111. To do this, they note that ∣∣W∣∣2||W||_2∣∣W∣∣2​ is the largest singular value of WWW, which may be estimated using the power method. The authors calculate both the dominant left and right singular vectors u1u_1u1​ and v1v_1v1​ of WWW and then estimate σ1≈uTWv\sigma_1 \approx u^T W vσ1​≈uTWv, but I don’t see any problem with performing the normal power method (for eigenvalues) on WTWW^T WWTW.
  • This approach to be both mathematically elegant and computationally inexpensive. The only problem I see is that bounding the spectral norm of each layer does not tightly bound the Lipschitz constant of the entire network. But perhaps in practice the bound is tight enough.
  • The images generated using spectral normalization seem really impressive!
